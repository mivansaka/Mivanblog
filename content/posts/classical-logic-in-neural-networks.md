As neural networks achieve superhuman performance on an ever-expanding range of tasks, a curious fact remains: we don't really know how they work. Or more precisely, we don't know *why* they work.

## The Black Box Problem

Neural networks are famously opaque. You train them, they achieve remarkable results, but explaining their internal decision-making remains incredibly difficult.

This stands in stark contrast to classical logic systems, where inference rules are explicit and reasoning is transparent.

## Bridges Between Worlds

Recent research suggests these aren't mutually exclusive approaches:

- **Neuro-symbolic AI** combines neural learning with symbolic reasoning
- **Attention mechanisms** can be interpreted as soft logical inference
- **Transformer architectures** show patterns reminiscent of formal logic systems

Perhaps neural networks are learning something *like* classical logic, but expressed in a different language.

## The Future of Understanding

I believe we're moving toward a reconciliation:

> "The most powerful AI systems will be those that can learn from data *and* explain their reasoning in human terms."

This requires new architectures, new training methods, and new ways of thinking about intelligence itself.

The future of AI isn't just bigger models. It's *understandable* models.
